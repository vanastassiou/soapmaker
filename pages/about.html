Purpose

This recipe builder helps soapmakers formulate cold process and hot process soap recipes. It calculates lye and water amounts, predicts soap properties based on fatty acid chemistry, and helps balance recipes for desired characteristics like hardness, lather, and skin conditioning.

The tool is designed for both beginners learning soap chemistry and experienced soapmakers who want to understand the science behind their formulations.

How calculations work

Core chemistry

Saponification calculations use established chemistry. Each fat has a SAP value (saponification value) representing how much lye is needed per gram of fat. These values are derived from average triglyceride molecular weights and are well-documented in chemical literature.

The basic formula is: Lye = (fat weight × SAP value) × (1 - superfat%)

Soap properties

Soap properties (hardness, lather, conditioning) are predicted by summing specific fatty acids in your recipe:

  • Hardness - saturated fatty acids (lauric, palmitic, stearic, etc.)
  • Cleansing - short-chain fatty acids (lauric, myristic)
  • Conditioning - unsaturated fatty acids (oleic, linoleic)
  • Lather - combination of lauric, myristic, and ricinoleic acids

The chemistry behind these correlations is sound: saturated fatty acids pack tightly creating harder bars, while short-chain acids produce more soluble sodium salts with stronger detergent action.

Recommended ranges

The recommended property ranges (hardness 29-54, cleansing 12-22, etc.) are empirically derived from soapmaking community experience, not from peer-reviewed chemical studies. They represent values that soapmakers have found produce good results in practice.

These ranges are attributed to SoapCalc, the widely-used community soapmaking calculator.

Data sources

This tool prioritizes authoritative sources following a clear hierarchy:

Tier 1: Peer-reviewed and government sources

  • USDA FoodData Central - fatty acid profiles for fats and oils (US Department of Agriculture)
  • PubChem - chemical compound data including SAP values and densities (NIH/NCBI)
  • NIST - unit conversion standards and statistical methods (National Institute of Standards and Technology)
  • Peer-reviewed journals - Journal of the American Oil Chemists' Society, Clinics in Dermatology

Tier 2: Professional standards

  • AOCS - analytical methods for fats, oils, and lipids (American Oil Chemists' Society)
  • IFRA - fragrance safety guidelines and usage limits (International Fragrance Association)
  • CRC Handbook - physical and chemical constants

Tier 3: Practice-based sources

  • SoapCalc - empirical property ranges derived from soapmaking community experience (explicitly acknowledged as non-peer-reviewed)
  • Scientific Soapmaking by Kevin Dunn - academic chemist's guide to soapmaking science

All sources are documented in the references page with publisher information, funding sources, and access details.

Transparency and limitations

This tool strives to be transparent about what is established chemistry versus what is empirical knowledge:

  • Established chemistry: SAP values, fatty acid chemistry, iodine values, density calculations
  • Empirical/community-derived: property threshold ranges, recipe optimization heuristics
  • Controversial: the INS value (SAP minus iodine) is included but noted as controversial - excellent soaps can fall outside the "ideal" range

Soap property predictions are guidelines, not guarantees. Actual results depend on many factors including technique, curing time, water quality, and ingredient freshness.

Technical details

This is a client-side web application with no server backend. All calculations run in your browser. Your recipes are saved to your browser's local storage and never transmitted anywhere.

The source code is available for review. All formulas are documented in the formulas reference with mathematical notation, worked examples, and citations.

Quality assurance

The calculator has been audited for:

  • Scientific validity of formulas
  • Source verification (URLs checked for accessibility and accuracy)
  • Appropriate attribution of empirical versus scientific values

Key findings: core chemistry calculations are valid and correctly implemented; empirical values are clearly attributed; sources are appropriately prioritized with peer-reviewed and government sources taking precedence.
